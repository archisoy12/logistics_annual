01 - What went wrong?

A tale of two markets: oversupply and vacancy

The early-2020s supply boom created a structural imbalance, but the impact varied sharply by asset type and quality.

The supply glut created by COVID-19

During the pandemic, optimistic e-commerce demand forecasts and a low-interest-rate environment drove an unprecedented logistics development cycle. From 2021 to 2023, new deliveries exceeded 2 million sqm annually, while e-commerce growth slowed to single digits from 2022 onward.

Distressed sales dominated the investment market

The leasing shock quickly moved into the capital market. As rates rose, cap-rate spreads narrowed and distressed transactions became a defining feature of the 2023-2024 period.

Conditions for a market inflection

By 2025, the major headwinds began to resolve: new supply declined sharply, occupier demand shifted toward higher-quality surviving assets, and arm's length transactions recovered as distressed sales plummeted.

Vacancy figures alone do not explain the market. Prime dry assets and non-core cold storage are moving on very different timelines.

Gyeonggi Province regulation changes the development equation

The ordinance tightens permit criteria for logistics facilities by applying a 400m setback from residential and noise-sensitive areas, requiring direct road connectivity of at least 12m width, and restricting building length to 150m or less in industrial and administrative zones. This makes large single-story, automation-ready development structurally harder in Gyeonggi Province.

Supply implication: demand remains concentrated in the GSA while new supply faces both physical and regulatory constraints, increasing the scarcity value of existing prime assets.
03 - Demand overview

The e-commerce market beyond quantitative growth

The demand story is shifting from volume expansion to operational efficiency, automation and network optimisation.

Domestic consumption is slow, but logistics remains strategic

Total online shopping transaction value reached KRW272 trillion in 2025. Excluding intangible services, online penetration reached around 54%, indicating a mature market where logistics efficiency becomes a key driver of corporate profitability.

Automation demand is becoming structural

Demographic pressure and rising labour costs mean that occupiers are focused on processing more goods with fewer staff. Automation, hub consolidation and workflow design are no longer optional investments.

Three shifts in logistics demand

ScaleLarge floor plates, high specifications and efficient circulation for automation.
DecentralisationExpansion toward Southern Gyeonggi and Chungcheong as occupiers balance cost, labour and connectivity.
Urban infillQuick commerce and instant delivery increase demand for city-adjacent nodes.

Leasing market read-through

The dry and cold warehouse charts now use the same company color palette across the newsletter. In dry warehouses, South and East improved meaningfully, while North and West remained more elevated. In cold storage, vacancy remained substantially higher across every submarket, especially when new builds are included.

At the GSA level, comparable-asset vacancy improved in both dry and cold segments, but the inclusion of newly delivered assets still lifts the market-wide figure. This is particularly important in cold storage, where the gap between existing-asset vacancy and new-build-inclusive vacancy remains wide, reflecting delayed lease-up and persistent oversupply in selected assets.

Nominal rents continued to edge higher, but this should not be read as a full recovery. Average GSA nominal rent in 2025 stood at KRW32,700 per 3.3 sqm for dry warehouses and KRW60,590 for cold storage, while new assets commanded premiums of KRW36,200 and KRW63,600 respectively.

Foreign Investor Continues to Drive Investment Trends

Foreign participation has been rising over time. The share remained below one-third in 2019–2022, increased to roughly one-third in 2023, climbed further in 2024, and then rose sharply in 2025.

In 2025, foreign capital was involved directly or indirectly in 15 of the 27 transactions completed, representing approximately KRW3.5 trillion of volume, or around 74% of total transaction value. The buyer base included KKR, GIC, Blackstone, Oaktree Capital, Warburg Pincus and M&G Real Estate.

Cross-border capital is no longer limited to straightforward equity acquisitions. Capital is entering through joint ventures, loan funds and pre-purchases, indicating that overseas investors remain active even as selectivity rises.

Core assets are expected to dominate as selectivity persists

A substantial volume of assets is expected to come to market in 2026 due to fund maturities, but actual closings may remain selective due to wide bid-ask spreads, vacancy risk and funding-cost volatility. Investors are likely to favour assets with proven occupancy, stronger specifications and clearer income visibility.

2026 outlook

Recovery is underway, but the market will remain selective

In 2025, the GSA logistics market passed a structural turning point as it continued to absorb the effects of oversupply. New supply declined sharply, distressed transactions fell, and demand for high-quality assets became more visible. The next phase is unlikely to be a uniform recovery; it will be led by prime, large-scale and operationally competitive assets.
